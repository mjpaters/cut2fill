## 1. Executive Summary

Cut2Fill is a digital platform that connects the construction earthworks industry — contractors, trucking companies, quarries, landfills, recyclers, councils, and government — giving every stakeholder visibility over what fill material is available, where, and in what condition.

Today, fill sourcing and disposal decisions are constrained by whoever you've engaged. A trucking company knows their sources. An earthworks sub knows theirs. But nobody has an industry-wide view. The result: trucks travel further than necessary, sit idle waiting for distant fill sites, and reusable material goes to landfill because the person who needed it didn't know it existed.

The deeper problem is classification. Most excavated material gets lumped as "clean fill" at best — but general fill varies enormously. Without proper testing and classification, material that could be select fill, structural fill, or a specific engineering grade gets treated as the lowest common denominator. Cut2Fill aims to change that by standardising how fill is classified, educating generators on the value of testing their material, and making it possible to match material to the right destination based on its actual properties — not just "someone needs dirt."

This is an engineering-grade problem. Construction projects need consistency. Even good material that's variable can cause issues on an engineered project. By requiring minimum 100 m³ listings (which typically come from a single source, reducing variability), integrating material classification guidance, and building toward standardised testing frameworks, Cut2Fill brings structure to what is currently chaos.

The platform integrates fire ant biosecurity zone awareness, registered facility mapping, material classification compliance, and logistics intelligence into every decision.

### The Opportunity

- **No industry-wide visibility exists.** Each company operates within its own network. Competitors like ReadyFill attempt to connect generators and receivers but rely on user-input classification with no quality control — resulting in a mixed bag of materials that can't be relied upon for engineering purposes.
- **General fill is the chaos point.** Engineered material (road base, structural fill from quarries) is specified and sourced upfront — that decision is fixed. General fill is where the waste, the inefficiency, and the cost blowouts live.
- **Better classification creates value.** If a generator tests their material properly, they can commoditise it — selling select fill or specific grades rather than giving away "clean fill." Without testing, there may also be contamination that nobody is aware of, creating liability. The incentive structure is broken: generators aren't motivated to spend money upfront on testing, but the industry pays for it downstream.
- **Waste levies punish disposal.** The QLD waste levy for general waste in the SEQ metro zone is $125/tonne (2025-26), rising $10/year to $145/tonne by 2027-28 [QLD Govt, Levy Rates](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/circular-economy-waste-reduction/disposal-levy/about/levy-rates). Clean earth sent to landfill as waste attracts the full levy rate since the exemption was removed on 1 July 2023 [QLD Govt FAQ](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/circular-economy-waste-reduction/policy-legislation/changes/frequently-asked-questions). Material accepted as day cover under an operational purposes exemption avoids the levy, but this option is limited and at the landfill operator's discretion.
- **No existing End of Waste (EOW) code** for soil/fill in Queensland — a regulatory gap that forces conservative disposal decisions.
- **Brisbane 2032 Olympics** driving unprecedented SEQ construction activity across venues, transport, and athlete village infrastructure.

### Revenue Model

"Free" means it costs nothing to explore, search, and find what you need. The platform generates revenue from suppliers, facilitated transactions, and data.

| Phase | Revenue Stream | Target |
|-------|---------------|--------|
| Year 1 | Free to explore — supplier enhanced profiles, listing fees | 200 registered companies, early supplier revenue |
| Year 2 | Supplier subscriptions, commodity data, managed brokerage for large projects | $180K ARR |
| Year 3 | Transaction facilitation (invoice intermediation), compliance documentation, government data licensing | $750K ARR |

**Future: Payment facilitation.** A structural barrier to material reuse is that contractors can't easily pay non-registered sources for material. Haulage companies solve this by bundling material cost into a per-m³ delivered rate with standard BCIPA-compliant payment terms — they're providing a financial intermediation service disguised as a service contract. Cut2Fill can become this intermediary digitally: invoice the contractor as a registered business (ABN, GST, standard terms), pay the source minus a facilitation fee. This removes the procurement barrier and creates a genuine competitive moat. Requires further analysis of trust account requirements, BCIPA compliance, and working capital. Two paths: (1) organic growth — fund from platform revenue when volume justifies it, or (2) investor-funded — raise capital to provide the float. Deferred to Phase 2-3.

## 2. Problem Statement

### 2.1 The Visibility Problem

South East Queensland's construction boom (driven by population growth of ~102,000/year [QGSO, Population Estimates 2024](https://www.qgso.qld.gov.au/statistics/theme/population/population-household-characteristics/growth-highlights), Brisbane 2032 infrastructure, and continuing Cross River Rail legacy works) generates enormous volumes of excavated material. Currently:

- **No industry-wide platform** connects material generators with receivers. Each company — contractors, trucking operators, earthworks subs — operates within its own network silo.
- **Existing alternatives are inadequate.** ReadyFill (readyfill.com.au) and Facebook groups attempt this but rely on user-input classification with no quality control, resulting in unreliable material descriptions that can't be trusted for engineering purposes.
- **Trucking companies limit your options.** When you engage a haulage or earthworks company, their known sources become your only sources. If their fill sites are 40 km away, that's where your trucks go — even if compatible material is available 5 km from your site.
- **Trucks are inefficient.** Standing idle waiting for fill sites to open, travelling further than necessary, empty running on return legs. Transport cost often exceeds material cost on short-haul earthworks.
- **Reusable material goes to waste** because the person who needed it didn't know it was available.

### 2.2 The Classification Problem

The earthworks industry has a classification problem that compounds the visibility problem:

- Most excavated material defaults to "clean fill" — the lowest common denominator classification — regardless of its actual properties.
- Without testing, material that could be select fill, a specific engineering grade, or even contaminated soil all gets treated the same.
- **Generators aren't motivated to test.** Testing costs money upfront, and if you're trying to get rid of material, there's no immediate incentive to characterise it properly. But without testing, you can't commoditise it.
- **The sale is: test your material, and it becomes a product.** Properly classified fill can be matched to projects that need that specific grade. Unclassified fill is just dirt that might go to landfill.
- **Unknown contamination is a hidden risk.** Without testing, generators and receivers are both exposed — either to regulatory liability or to engineering problems on the receiving project.
- **Engineering requires consistency.** Even good material that varies batch to batch can cause problems. Variable fill from multiple unknown sources is an engineering risk. This is why Cut2Fill requires minimum 100 m³ — larger quantities from a single source have more consistent properties.

### 2.3 The Cost Problem

The economic penalty for poor fill decisions is compounding:

- **QLD waste levy (metro zone):** $125/tonne in 2025-26, rising to $145/tonne by 2027-28 — increasing $10/year [QLD Govt, Levy Rates](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/circular-economy-waste-reduction/disposal-levy/about/levy-rates)
- **Clean earth exemption removed** 1 July 2023 — clean earth disposed to landfill now attracts the full general waste levy rate [QLD Govt FAQ](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/circular-economy-waste-reduction/policy-legislation/changes/frequently-asked-questions)
- **Day cover exemption exists** — landfill operators can apply for operational purposes exemption for material used as day cover, access roads, or capping. Day cover rates (~$35/m³) are significantly below the full levy rate, but this is at the operator's discretion and limited by site needs.
- **Transport costs dominate** — on short-haul earthworks, truck hire and fuel often exceed the material cost. Every unnecessary kilometre compounds the problem.
- **10.25 million tonnes** of headline waste generated in QLD (2024-25), with C&D waste achieving 83.3% diversion from landfill (2023-24) against a 75% target [QLD State of the Environment 2024](https://www.stateoftheenvironment.detsi.qld.gov.au/pollution/waste/construction-and-demolition-waste-landfilled)

### 2.4 The Compliance Problem

Moving earthworks material in Queensland involves overlapping regulatory requirements that are difficult for non-specialists to navigate:

| Regulation | Trigger | Consequence of Non-Compliance |
|-----------|---------|-------------------------------|
| EP Act 1994 s.319 | General environmental duty — ALL earthworks | Civil and criminal penalties |
| EP Act 1994 s.424-425 | Moving soil from EMR/CLR listed land | 100 penalty units ($16,692) per offence |
| Biosecurity Act 2014 s.23 | Moving soil within/between fire ant zones | 750 penalty units ($125,175) individuals; 3,750 ($625,875) corporations |
| EP Regulation 2019 ERA 60 | Disposing of waste at unlicensed site | Prosecution, remediation orders |
| State Planning Policy (ASS) | Excavation ≥100m³ below 5m AHD | Stop-work orders, remediation |
| Local government | Fill placement without approval | Compliance notices, fines |

The penalty for moving soil across a fire ant biosecurity zone boundary without a Biosecurity Instrument Permit (BIP) is **$125,175 for an individual and $625,875 for a corporation** (based on QLD penalty unit value of $166.90, effective 1 July 2025; [QLD Govt](https://www.dlgwv.qld.gov.au/local-government/for-councils/laws/value-of-a-penalty-unit)). Yet there is no digital tool that alerts a project manager when a material exchange would cross zone boundaries.

### 2.5 The Information Problem

Critical data exists but is fragmented across multiple government systems:

- **Environmental Authorities Register** — lists all ERA-licensed facilities but has no coordinates (text addresses only)
- **QLD Waste Facilities Dataset** — has coordinates but last updated October 2018
- **Mining permits** — available via ArcGIS REST API but requires GIS expertise to query
- **Fire ant zones** — updated monthly via QLD Open Data but not integrated into any industry tool
- **EMR/CLR registers** — searchable only via DES website, no API

No single platform consolidates this information into an actionable tool for the construction industry.

## 3. Solution

### 3.1 Platform Overview

Cut2Fill is a web-based platform (responsive, mobile-ready) with the following core capabilities:

**1. Registered Facility Mapping** *(built)*
- 604 verified SEQ facilities: 488 quarries, 50 concrete suppliers, 44 transfer stations, 10 landfills, 7 soil treatment, 3 C&D recyclers, 2 PFAS treatment
- Interactive Leaflet.js map with facility type filtering, search, and detail panels
- Fire ant biosecurity zone overlay (Zone 1 restricted + Zone 2 buffer) with point-in-polygon zone checking
- LGA boundary overlay for all SEQ councils
- Address search with geocoding

**2. Compliance Intelligence** *(partially built)*
- Fire ant biosecurity zone checking on every facility and location *(built)*
- Material classification guidance with QLD-specific regulatory references *(built — material tooltips)*
- Zone crossing warnings when matching listings across different biosecurity zones *(planned — requires material exchange)*
- Links to relevant permits, forms, and government resources *(planned)*

**3. Water Fill Points** *(built)*
- 83+ SEQ water fill stations mapped (QUU, Unitywater, Gold Coast CC, Logan CC, Redland CC)
- Provider, access type, and water type (potable/recycled) for each station
- Live API fallback to backend database

**4. Major Projects Pipeline** *(built)*
- Brisbane 2032 Olympic and SEQ infrastructure projects with location, cost, authority, and timeline
- Project phases with start/end dates and progress tracking
- Filterable by status and project type

**5. Logistics & Emissions Calculator** *(built)*
- Three truck profiles (body truck, truck & dog, semi tipper) with payload and fuel data
- Trip count, fuel consumption, CO₂ emissions, and time estimates
- Haversine distance with road factor correction

**6. Submissions & Admin** *(built)*
- User-submitted site registrations, source tips, and feedback via backend API
- Admin panel for submission review, facility CRUD, source health checks, and user management
- User dashboard with submission tracking

**7. Material Exchange** *(planned — Phase 2)*
- Post listings for material available (surplus) or material wanted (demand)
- Minimum 100m³ per listing — professional scale only
- Smart matching algorithm considers material type, volume, location proximity, date overlap, and zone compatibility
- Two pricing tiers: **Free** (material available at no cost) or **Quote Required** (commercial arrangement)

### 3.2 Target Users

| User Type | Need | Cut2Fill Value |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| **Tier 1-3 Contractors** | Dispose of surplus material quickly, cheaply, compliantly | Find receivers near their site, avoid $125/t levy, compliance guidance |
| **Civil Earthworks Companies** | Source fill material for projects | Find verified supply, reduce virgin quarry dependence |
| **Local Councils** | Monitor material flows, support circular economy targets | Dashboard data, facilitate reuse within LGA |
| **State Government (DES, TMR, BQCC)** | Track material movements, enforce compliance | Platform data informs policy, zone compliance built-in |
| **Licensed Facilities** | Attract customers, fill capacity | Permanent listings, verified badges, visibility |
| **Developers** | Manage earthworks costs on large projects | Find competitive fill/disposal options with compliance certainty |

### 3.3 What Cut2Fill Is NOT

- Not a marketplace for small residential loads (<100m³)
- Not a waste broker or transport company
- Not a testing laboratory or certification body
- Not a permit issuer — we provide guidance and links to government services

### 4.3 Regulatory Tailwinds

1. **Waste levy escalation** — $125/t (2025-26) → $135/t (2026-27) → $145/t (2027-28), then CPI-indexed. Every year, the economic case for reuse strengthens. [QLD Govt, Levy Rates](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/circular-economy-waste-reduction/disposal-levy/about/levy-rates)
2. **Clean earth exemption removal** (1 July 2023) — previously, clean earth was exempt from the waste levy. Its removal created an entirely new cost pressure. Note: material accepted as day cover at landfills can be exempt under operational purposes provisions. [QLD Govt FAQ](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/circular-economy-waste-reduction/policy-legislation/changes/frequently-asked-questions)
3. **The levy is a behavioural tool, not a tax.** The QLD Government's stated policy intent is to incentivise diversion, not generate revenue. 70% of levy revenue ($428.8M in 2023-24) is committed to reinvestment in waste management and resource recovery. This means the government is actively seeking platforms and programs that help achieve diversion outcomes — Cut2Fill is directly aligned. [QLD Govt, About the Levy](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/circular-economy-waste-reduction/disposal-levy/about/overview)
4. **$2.1 billion "Recycling Revolution" package** funded from levy revenue (10-year). Includes $1.1B Recycling and Jobs Fund for waste avoidance, recycling infrastructure, and circular economy programs. Multiple active grant programs with open applications. [$2.1B Announcement](https://statements.qld.gov.au/statements/94068)
5. **Government precedent for endorsing digital platforms.** QLD Government invested $2.4M in Recycle Mate (private digital platform for waste/recycling). ASPIRE (CSIRO material exchange platform) adopted by 5+ SEQ councils with 7,152+ tonnes diverted. Cut2Fill is a more sophisticated, construction-specific version of what these platforms do generally. [Recycle Mate](https://www.qld.gov.au/environment/waste-reduction-recycling/funding-grants/recycle-mate)
6. **New QLD Waste Strategy (2025-2030) being developed now.** The current strategy's C&D diversion target (75%) is already exceeded at 83.3%. New, more ambitious targets will need platforms like Cut2Fill to achieve them. This is the policy window for engagement.
7. **QLD Recycling Modernisation Fund (QRMF)** — successor to the RRIDP (now closed). [QLD State Development](https://www.statedevelopment.qld.gov.au/industry/critical-industry-support/resource-recovery)
8. **No EOW code for soil/fill** — this regulatory gap means there is no streamlined pathway for declaring excavated soil as "not waste." Cut2Fill can advocate for and facilitate the development of such a code.
9. **Brisbane 2032 sustainability commitments** — the Olympics bid included commitments to circular economy and sustainable construction.
10. **Road safety co-benefits.** Heavy vehicles are 2% of registered vehicles but involved in ~15% of QLD road fatalities. TMR co-funds the CLOCS-A standard for construction logistics safety. Every material match on Cut2Fill that shortens a haul distance or eliminates a landfill trip is a measurable road safety outcome. [TMR Road Safety](https://www.tmr.qld.gov.au/safety/road-safety/road-safety-policy)

**Future opportunity: Levy credit scheme.** There is currently no mechanism for contractors to claim levy credits for demonstrating waste diversion. But the logic is sound: the levy incentivises diversion → Cut2Fill facilitates and documents diversion → the platform's audit trail could serve as verification for a credit scheme. This would require policy advocacy but aligns with the levy's stated purpose.

## 5. Technology & Data Strategy

### 5.1 Architecture

**Frontend:** Single-page application (HTML/CSS/JavaScript) — *deployed to GitHub Pages*
- Leaflet.js for mapping (open source, no API key required)
- Responsive design for field use on tablets/phones
- No framework dependency — vanilla JS (2,500+ lines in `app.js`)
- Supabase JS client for authentication
- Dark theme with CSS custom properties

**Backend:** FastAPI (Python 3.12) — *deployed to Render via Docker*
- Async SQLAlchemy with asyncpg for PostgreSQL/PostGIS
- Supabase JWT auth via JWKS validation, admin role system
- 7 API routers: facilities, water points, projects, submissions, profile, admin, health
- Rate limiting (60 req/min per IP via slowapi)
- CORS middleware for cross-origin frontend access
- 2 Alembic migrations (initial schema + foreign keys)

**Database:** Supabase-hosted PostgreSQL with PostGIS
- Models: Facility, WaterFillPoint, MajorProject, ProjectPhase, Submission, Profile
- GIST index on geometry columns, connection pooled (max 10 + 5 overflow)

**Data Integration:**

### 5.2 Matching Algorithm *(Planned — Phase 2)*

The matching engine will consider:
1. **Material compatibility** — type match (e.g., clean fill available → clean fill wanted)
2. **Volume overlap** — minimum viable exchange quantity
3. **Proximity** — distance between sites (driving distance, not straight line)
4. **Date overlap** — availability windows must intersect
5. **Zone compatibility** — flag if exchange crosses fire ant biosecurity zone boundary
6. **Compliance requirements** — flag if material type requires permits (contaminated soil, ASS)

*Note: The logistics & emissions calculator (truck profiles, fuel, CO₂, trip estimates) is already built and will feed into proximity scoring when the matching engine is implemented.*

### 5.3 Data Moat

Over time, Cut2Fill accumulates:
- Historical material flow data across SEQ
- Pricing intelligence (market rates for different materials)
- Seasonal patterns in supply and demand
- Transport corridor utilisation
- Facility capacity and utilisation rates

This data becomes increasingly valuable to government for policy making and to industry for project planning.
