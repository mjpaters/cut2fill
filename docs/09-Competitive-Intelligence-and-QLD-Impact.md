## PART 1: GLOBAL COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

### The Short Answer

**No platform in the world combines what Cut2Fill is building.** Platforms exist for dirt matching (USA), material passports (Netherlands), soil tracking (Canada), and building material reuse (Nordics) — but none combine material exchange, regulatory compliance, cost comparison, supply chain carbon mapping, and government data monetisation in a single free platform.

**In Australia specifically, there is nothing.** The two existing options (Fillsites, Fill Wanted) are bare-bones classifieds boards with no mapping, no compliance, no intelligence, and no scale threshold.

### Competitor Deep Dive

#### Tier 1: Closest Competitors (Earthworks Material Exchange)

**1. Soil Connect (USA)** — soilconnect.com *(updated April 2026 from deep dive)*

**Overview:** The best-funded player in this space globally. SaaS-enabled marketplace combined with managed dirt brokerage services. Founded 2018, HQ Plainview NY. New sales-focused CEO (Jonathan Alvarado, ex-TRUX) appointed Jan 2025 — signals they're in scale mode, not build mode.

**Scale:** 40M+ cubic yards matched (2024), 30M+ cubic yards in first half of 2025. Users save 34% on average ($5/yard). Managed brokerage saves clients avg $128,125 per engagement. ~18 employees (North America + Asia). Expanding into 9 new metro markets (NC, SC, CO, FL, CA, AZ, GA).

**Funding:** $11.9M total raised.
| Round | Date | Amount | Lead |
|-------|------|--------|------|
| Pre-seed | ~2019 | $750K | Unknown |
| Seed | Dec 2020 | $3.25M | TIA Ventures / Heartland Ventures (co-lead) |
| Strategic | Sep 2021 | Undisclosed | Heartland Ventures |
| Venture | Jan 2023 | ~$5.58M | Unknown |
| Latest | Jan 2025 | ~$2.3M | Heartland Ventures |

Strategic investors include CEMEX Ventures (world's largest building materials company) and Suffolk Technologies (Suffolk Construction VC arm).

**Revenue model:** SaaS subscriptions — NOT transaction fees. Their terms explicitly state they "do not charge any fees or receive any commissions on transactions between end users." Free tier for basic marketplace. Paid tiers for e-ticketing, production management, commodity data, and managed brokerage.

**Key features:**
- Proximity-based material matching
- E-ticketing / digital load tracking (GPS, photo capture, eSignatures)
- Patent-pending eRegulatory module (NY Metro only) — captures truck/load/route data for compliance documentation
- Dirt Production Management (real-time volume/schedule/budget tracking)
- Dirt Commodity Market Data (pricing intelligence, subscription)
- Preconstruction tool (beta 2025, for estimators — real-time dirt market rate forecasting)
- QuickStart program (builds supplier profiles for them to claim — solves chicken-and-egg)
- Procore integration

**Critical gaps (Cut2Fill opportunities):**
1. **Does NOT handle payments.** Terms explicitly state they don't facilitate transactions. Buyer and seller sort out payment themselves. The procurement barrier for contractors paying non-registered sources is unsolved.
2. **No material testing/lab integration.** No contamination verification, no quality assurance.
3. **Compliance limited to NY Metro.** eRegulatory is a bolt-on for one geography, not core.
4. **No sustainability/carbon tracking.** Despite CEMEX Ventures' sustainability framing.
5. **No self-serve pricing.** Enterprise sales motion — must "request a demo."
6. **No cut/fill volume calculation.** Purely a marketplace, no earthworks estimation.
7. **Lean team risk.** ~18 employees across marketplace, brokerage, data, compliance, and production management.

**Takeaway:** Proves the market at scale and validates the business model. But their deliberate choice not to handle payments is a genuine strategic gap. Australia's uniform regulatory environment makes compliance a stronger moat here than in the fragmented US landscape. Soil Connect's subscription model (not transaction fees) is worth noting — they monetise access and tools, not individual trades.

**2. Nexus ReGen / Soil Link (UK)** — nexusregen.com / soil.link
- AI-driven materials exchange for heavy construction (soils, asphalt, aggregates, concrete)
- AI reads and summarises technical reports for smart matching
- Claims 26% average savings for members
- Seed funding from SK Consulting & Investment
- **What they DON'T do:** No regulatory mapping, no biosecurity, no contaminated land overlays, no cost calculators, no carbon supply chain mapping, no government data play, no tender reporting
- **Takeaway:** Their AI document parsing is strong and aligns with Cut2Fill's Phase 3 geotech report upload feature. Worth watching their approach.

**3. Earth Exchange (UK)** — earth-exchange.com
- Map-driven portal for exchanging soils and aggregates in North-West England
- Interesting angle: also trades Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) units
- Works under CL:AIRE DoWCoP (UK's equivalent of clean fill compliance framework)
- Small monthly subscription fee, no brokerage
- Regional scale, appears modest
- **What they DON'T do:** No AI matching, no carbon tracking, no cost calculators, no automated quoting, no government data
- **Takeaway:** BNG trading is an interesting adjacent market. Australia's EPBC Act reforms may create similar biodiversity credit opportunities.

#### Tier 2: Adjacent Platforms (Broader Circular Economy / Potential Partners)

**4. ASPIRE** (aspiresme.com) — CSIRO spinout, B-Corp *(updated April 2026 from deep dive)*

General-purpose circular economy waste exchange platform. Council-funded model — councils buy enterprise licences, local businesses get free access. Spun out of CSIRO Data61 as ASPIRE PL. CEO Cameron McKenzie.

- **Materials:** General industrial/commercial waste — batteries, plastics, textiles, organics, metals, chemicals. Soil/spoil listed as a minor category among many. NOT construction-specific.
- **Scale:** 3,500+ registered businesses across 20+ councils in VIC/QLD/NSW. Sunshine Coast: 434 members, 7,152t diverted since Oct 2020 (tiny by construction standards — a single earthworks project can exceed this).
- **QLD councils:** Sunshine Coast, Moreton Bay, Redland, Gold Coast, Logan, Brisbane.
- **How it works:** Users enter resource profiles (inputs/outputs). Algorithm matches businesses. Active notifications, not just passive browsing. Real-time impact dashboard.
- **Technology:** Web + mobile app. Algorithm-driven matching. ASPIRE+ adds LCA carbon tracking. Reasonably modern but not cutting-edge.
- **Pricing:** Councils pay enterprise licence. Local businesses (typically <100 FTE) get free access.
- **Construction features:** None. No fire ant zones, no material quality specs, no volume management, no haulage optimisation, no regulatory compliance, no project timelines.
- **Not a direct competitor.** The overlap is narrow — only where someone lists excess soil on ASPIRE, which is a tiny fraction of what the platform handles.
- **Partnership opportunity is strong.** ASPIRE has existing council relationships across SEQ. Cut2Fill as the construction-specific vertical, ASPIRE as the general circular economy platform. Joint council pitches, cross-referral, shared impact reporting. They have an active [partnership program](https://aspiresme.com/aspire-partnership-program-empower-businesses-together/).

**5. Rubbl** (rubbl.com) — NOT a materials marketplace *(clarified April 2026)*

Rubbl is a **heavy equipment rental marketplace** (Airbnb for excavators/skid steers). It does NOT handle dirt, fill, soil, or aggregates. $1.3-2M seed funding, ~18 employees, Houston TX. Not a competitor in the materials space. Note: there is a separate, unrelated Australian company "RUBBL Technologies" (rubbl.app) that handles bulk landscape materials — different entity.

**6. Excess Materials Exchange (Netherlands)** — excessmaterialsexchange.com
- B2B marketplace for ANY excess materials (not construction-specific)
- AI-powered matching, Product Passports, blockchain for data security
- Claims 60% environmental impact reduction, 110% increase in material financial value
- $5M revenue, $5M Series A (Dec 2024, Wa'ed Ventures)
- Partnered with Irish Green Building Council for Construction Materials Exchange (CMEx)
- **Not a direct competitor** — broader than earthworks, not geo-specific, no mapping
- **Takeaway:** Their Product Passport concept and blockchain approach are interesting for material traceability. Their EU expansion strategy is worth studying.

**5. Madaster (Netherlands/Global)** — madaster.com
- Material passport platform for buildings and infrastructure
- Records all materials/products IN buildings with composition, quality, origin, disassembly info
- Circularity scoring, embodied carbon calculations, residual value reporting
- Operating in 10+ countries (Germany, UK, Belgium, Switzerland, Portugal, Canada, Dubai, China, Singapore)
- Supervised by not-for-profit Madaster Foundation, EU Horizon 2020 grant ~EUR2.5M
- **Not a competitor** — tracks materials in permanent structures, not earthworks flows
- **Takeaway:** Complementary. Material passports for buildings + Cut2Fill for earthworks = complete construction circular economy tracking.

### 2.2 Waste Levy — The $125-145M/Year Problem

The removal of the clean earth levy exemption on 1 July 2023 changed everything:

| Year | Metro Levy Rate | Estimated Reusable Material to Landfill | Levy Cost |
|------|----------------|----------------------------------------|-----------|
| 2024-25 | $125/t | 571,000 tonnes | $65.7M |
| 2025-26 | $125/t | 571,000 tonnes | $71.4M |
| 2027-28 | $145/t | 571,000 tonnes | $82.8M |

This is money being burned. Every tonne of reusable fill sent to landfill costs the QLD construction industry $125-145 in pure waste — material that another project would use for free if they knew it existed.

**Cut2Fill's impact at scale:**

| Diversion Rate | Tonnes Diverted | Levy Savings/Year | CO2 Avoided |
|---------------|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| 5% | 28,550 t | $3.3M-4.1M | ~750 t |
| 10% | 57,100 t | $6.6M-8.3M | ~1,500 t |
| 25% | 142,750 t | $16.4M-20.7M | ~3,750 t |
| 50% | 285,500 t | $32.8M-41.4M | ~7,500 t |

Even a modest 10% diversion rate saves the QLD construction industry **$6.6-8.3M per year** and avoids 1,500 tonnes of CO2.

### 2.3 Small Business — The Equity Argument

**96% of QLD construction businesses employ fewer than 20 people.**

Large Tier 1 contractors can self-match material across their own portfolio of projects. They have the networks, the logistics teams, and the volume to make it work. A small civil contractor with one project generating 5,000m3 of surplus has none of these advantages.

Currently, a small contractor's options:
1. **Send it to the tip** — pay $125/t, because they don't know who needs fill nearby
2. **Make phone calls** — burn hours calling around, hoping someone in their network knows someone
3. **Post on Fillsites** — basic listing, no matching, no compliance guidance, hope for the best

Cut2Fill gives that small contractor the same matching, cost comparison, compliance guidance, and market visibility that a Tier 1 contractor gets through its internal systems — for free.

**This is a levelling tool.** It reduces the cost disadvantage that small businesses face when dealing with earthworks waste. The $125/t levy hits a small contractor proportionally harder than a large one, and the information asymmetry (not knowing who needs your material) costs them the most.

### 2.4 Employment Impact

QLD faces an **18,200 average worker shortfall** over the next 8 years, peaking at **50,000 in 2026-27** (QMCA).

Cut2Fill doesn't directly create thousands of jobs, but it has three employment effects:

**1. Project budget freed up = more productive work funded**
Every dollar NOT spent on $125/t waste levy stays in the project budget. On a $50M road project, if Cut2Fill saves $500K in disposal costs, that's $500K available for actual construction work — which employs people.

**2. New roles in material treatment**
The treatment pathway (cut material → lime stabilisation → specification-compliant fill) creates demand for:
- Material testing technicians
- Treatment plant operators
- Logistics coordinators
- Environmental compliance officers

**3. Data and platform roles**
As Cut2Fill scales, the data products require:
- Data analysts (material flow intelligence)
- GIS specialists (mapping, spatial analysis)
- Customer success (government and enterprise accounts)

### 2.7 Carbon & Climate

QLD targets:
- 30% emissions reduction by 2030 (already at 29%)
- 75% by 2035
- Net zero by 2050

Construction produces **37-64 Mt CO2e/year nationally** (varies by estimation method). Material reuse avoids emissions in three ways:

| Emission Source | How Cut2Fill Reduces It |
|----------------|----------------------|
| **Transport** | Shorter haul to reuse site vs distant quarry or tip. Every truck-km avoided = 2.68 kg CO2/L diesel saved. |
| **Extraction** | Reused fill replaces virgin quarry material. Quarrying has significant embodied carbon (blasting, crushing, screening). |
| **Landfill** | Reduced truck movements to/from landfill. Reduced landfill operations. |

The supply chain carbon mapping feature (Section 9 of Product Decisions doc) takes this further — showing the full embedded carbon of material choices from raw extraction to project site.

Cut2Fill provides the **verification mechanism** for demonstrating material reuse on Olympic construction projects:

### 2.9 Government Savings — Illegal Dumping

**46 QLD councils investigated 20,000+ illegal dumping reports.** Illegal dumping is directly driven by high disposal costs — when the tip costs $125/t, some operators dump material illegally.

Cut2Fill reduces illegal dumping by making legal reuse cheaper and easier than illegal disposal. If the platform shows a free fill receiver 10km away, there's no economic incentive to dump illegally.

**Estimated council savings from reduced illegal dumping investigation and remediation: $5-20M/year across SEQ.** This is a direct argument for council data licensing — the platform pays for itself in reduced enforcement costs.

### 2.10 Innovation & Export Opportunity

**Global ConTech market: US$5.6B, growing to $10.3B by 2030.**

Brisbane is emerging as a ConTech hub — the FCON (Future of Construction) summit relocated to Brisbane in 2024. Cut2Fill positions QLD as a leader in circular economy construction technology.

**Export potential:**
- The platform model (free exchange + data monetisation) is replicable in any jurisdiction
- Regulatory compliance layer adapts to local regulations (fire ants are QLD-specific, but contaminated land, waste levies, and biosecurity exist everywhere)

- Victoria's "Recycled First" policy (1.3M tonnes recycled content in transport projects) shows demand — but they have no digital matching platform to enable it

**QLD could build this first, prove it at scale through Brisbane 2032, and export the model nationally and internationally.**

## PART 3: ADDITIONAL USE CASES FROM RESEARCH

### Use Case: Government Infrastructure Program Manager

**User:** TMR or council program manager overseeing multiple projects
**Situation:** Managing 15 road projects across SEQ simultaneously, each generating or requiring earthworks material
**Current approach:** Each project manages earthworks independently. Material from Project A goes to landfill while Project B, 12km away, imports from a quarry.
**Cut2Fill approach:** Program-level dashboard showing all projects' cut/fill positions. Automated matching across the portfolio. "Project A's surplus is Project B's import — save $1.2M."
**Data value:** Government sees cross-project optimisation opportunities they currently miss entirely.

### Use Case: Insurance/Risk Assessment

**User:** Construction insurer or risk assessor
**Situation:** Assessing environmental risk on a construction project — is the fill source compliant? Is contaminated material being managed properly?
**Cut2Fill approach:** Platform data shows material chain of custody, compliance certificates, zone crossing records
**Data value:** Insurers would pay for risk intelligence — "projects using Cut2Fill-tracked material have lower environmental incident rates"

### Use Case: Academic Research

**User:** University researcher studying construction material flows, circular economy, or urban metabolism
**Situation:** Needs real data on material movements in an Australian city — currently doesn't exist
**Cut2Fill approach:** Anonymised dataset of material flows across SEQ — types, volumes, distances, timing, reuse rates
**Data value:** $5-25K per dataset. CSIRO, QUT, UQ, Griffith would all use this.

### Use Case: Biodiversity/Environmental Offset

**User:** Developer with biodiversity offset obligations or contaminated land remediation requirements
**Situation:** Needs specific soil types for rehabilitation, revegetation, or capping contaminated sites
**Cut2Fill approach:** Find topsoil, clean fill for capping, or specific material for rehabilitation — matched from nearby projects
**Data value:** Links material exchange to environmental compliance outcomes

### Use Case: Disaster Recovery

**User:** Council or state emergency services after flood, cyclone, or landslide
**Situation:** Urgent need for fill material for stabilisation, levee repair, road reinstatement
**Cut2Fill approach:** Instantly identify available material nearby — no phone tag during a crisis
**Data value:** Disaster preparedness — pre-mapped material sources for emergency response plans
